About This Package

This is the Ladakh package for people who want to go further. The standard Leh circuit ends at Hunder. This one continues past Hunder to Turtuk, the last village in India on the road toward Pakistan, a place that did not open to tourists until 2010 and which has changed very little since. The extra day makes the difference between seeing Ladakh and actually feeling like you got somewhere remote.

Seven days in Ladakh is a proper amount of time. You have a day to acclimatise properly, a day to cover the Leh sights, a day to cross Khardungla and reach Nubra, a day to push out to Turtuk, a day to drive the Shyok Valley to Pangong, a day to cross Chang La back to Leh, and a day to fly home. Nothing is rushed and nothing important is skipped.

Day-by-Day Itinerary

Day 1
Arrival in Leh — Rest and Acclimatisation

Arrive at Leh Airport and transfer to your hotel. The altitude here is 3,500 metres and the body needs time to adjust before anything strenuous. The full day is free. Drink plenty of water, eat light, avoid alcohol, and sleep early. This day is not a waste. It is the reason the rest of the trip goes well. People who ignore the acclimatisation day tend to spend day three in bed with altitude sickness.

Day 2
Leh — Shyam Valley Sightseeing

A full day covering the major landmarks along the old Srinagar-Leh highway. The Hall of Fame museum near the airport is a thorough and well-presented record of the Indian Army in Ladakh, with personal accounts and equipment from the Kargil War and earlier campaigns. Gurudwara Pathar Sahib is quietly impressive, built around a boulder said to bear the imprint of Guru Nanak's palm after an encounter with a demon.

Magnetic Hill is the inevitable stop where everyone photographs their car appearing to roll uphill. Sangam, where the Indus and Zanskar rivers meet, is worth more time than most tourists give it. The two rivers run alongside each other for a visible distance before mixing, the olive Indus next to the grey-white Zanskar. Shanti Stupa at dusk, reached by a short drive and a climb of steps, looks out over the entire Leh valley.

Day 3
Leh → Khardungla Pass → Nubra Valley → Hunder

The drive to Nubra Valley via Khardungla at 18,380 feet is the one Ladakh experience that appears on every itinerary for good reason. The summit is cold and exposed and the view is enormous. The descent into the valley takes you from that desolate summit environment down into green, irrigated farmland in the space of a couple of hours. The contrast is one of the more dramatic things Ladakh offers.

Diskit Monastery on its promontory above the Nubra Valley floor is the oldest monastery in the region, founded in the 14th century. The 32-metre Maitreya Buddha below it gazes northward across the valley. Continue to Hunder, where cold desert geology has placed sand dunes improbably between the mountains. Bactrian double-humped camels, a remnant of the old Silk Route caravans, graze on the dunes. A camel ride at sunset in this landscape is a genuinely strange and memorable experience. Overnight in Hunder.

Day 4
Hunder → Turtuk → Thang Zero Point → Hunder

This day is the reason this package costs more and takes longer than a standard Leh tour, and it is worth every rupee and every hour. Turtuk is about two hours from Hunder along the Shyok River. The village only opened to civilian tourists in 2010 after decades of military restriction. It is Balti, which means it is culturally, linguistically, and architecturally distinct from everything else you will see in Ladakh. The lanes are narrow, the apricot and mulberry trees overhang stone walls, and the people who live here are descended from the same communities that exist across the border.

Beyond Turtuk, the road continues to Thang, also called Zero Point, the end of the road in India on this corridor. You are looking across into Pakistan from a distance of a few kilometres. The landscape here is stripped back and severe. Return to Hunder by evening for the second overnight in Nubra.

Day 5
Hunder → Pangong Lake via Shyok

The five-hour drive along the Shyok River to Pangong Tso is one of the most beautiful long drives in India. The valley is wide and the mountains on both sides are extraordinary: red, brown, purple, occasionally dusted with snow. There are almost no settlements. The road passes through Tangste village before the final climb to the lake.

Pangong Tso at 4,350 metres is 134 kilometres long and straddles the India-China border. The colour of the water is the thing that photographs cannot fully convey. It is very blue, with a specific quality of blue that changes throughout the day depending on the light and cloud. In the morning it tends toward deep cobalt. By midday it can shift toward turquoise. At its edges the water is clear enough to see the bottom several metres down. Camp overnight at the lakeside. Sitting outside your tent at Pangong at night, with the lake perfectly still and the stars visible without interference, is an experience worth making the trip for.

Day 6
Pangong → Leh via Chang La Pass

Wake early for the last morning at Pangong. The lake at 6am is different from the lake at noon, and you will not regret setting an alarm. After breakfast, the drive back toward Leh crosses Chang La at 17,585 feet, the third highest motorable road in the world.

The road back passes through some of the finest monastery country in Ladakh. Thiksey Monastery sits on a stepped hillside above the Indus Valley, built in layers like a small Tibetan town, with each level housing a different function. It was founded in the 15th century and is still active. Shey Palace and its monastery are a short detour, housing one of the largest seated Buddha statues in Ladakh. The 3 Idiots filming location at Rancho's School is nearby. Sindhu Darshan Ghat at the Indus is a calm final stop before the drive into Leh for the last overnight.

Day 7
Departure from Leh

Early morning transfer to Leh Airport. Seven days in Ladakh is enough to understand why people come back.

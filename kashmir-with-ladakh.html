Kashmir with Ladakh Tour

About This Package

Nine days is what it takes to do Jammu and Kashmir properly. This package does not shortcut anything. You spend two nights in Srinagar and actually see the city, a day in Gulmarg on one of the highest cable cars in the world, a night in Kargil on the road to Leh with a stop at the war memorial, the full Leh circuit including Khardungla, Nubra Valley, Turtuk village near the Pakistan border, and Pangong Lake. Then back to Leh via Chang La.

The transition between Kashmir and Ladakh on the Srinagar-Leh highway is one of the most visually striking things in India. You drive through the lush green valleys of Kashmir, cross Zoji La, and within a few kilometres the landscape becomes dry, barren, and extraordinary. This package lets you experience both ends of that contrast and everything in between.

Day-by-Day Itinerary

Day 1
Arrival in Srinagar — Mughal Gardens and Dal Lake

You land in Srinagar and are met at the airport. The afternoon is spent at the Mughal Gardens along Dal Lake. Shalimar Bagh, built by Emperor Jehangir in 1616 as a gift to his wife Nur Jahan, is the most formal of the three, symmetrical and terraced with old chinar trees down every path. Nishat Bagh next door is the largest of the Mughal gardens, with twelve terraces and the Zabarwan mountains rising directly behind it. Chashma Shahi is the smallest and most peaceful, fed by a natural spring.

The evening Shikara ride on Dal Lake is not optional. One hour on the water at dusk, passing through the floating gardens and the narrow channels between houseboats, is the correct introduction to Srinagar. Overnight at hotel or houseboat.

Day 2
Srinagar → Gulmarg → Srinagar

Gulmarg is 55 km from Srinagar. The drive takes about two hours and climbs steadily through pine forests. The meadow at 2,440 metres is wide and green in summer, covered in wildflowers through July, and completely white from November to March when it becomes India's premier ski destination. In summer the Gondola cable car is the main draw, climbing to Kongdoori at Phase 1 and then to Apharwat Peak at Phase 2, 4,200 metres, with views of the high Himalayan peaks in every direction. The world's highest golf course is here. Return to Srinagar by evening. Second overnight in Srinagar.

Gulmarg Meadow (2,440m)Gondola Cable Car Phase 1 and 2 (own cost)Apharwat Peak Views (4,200m)Khilanmarg Meadow
Day 3
Srinagar → Sonamarg → Drass → Kargil

An early start for a long and genuinely interesting drive. The route follows the Sindh River through Sonamarg (Meadow of Gold) at 2,740 metres, the last major stop before the high passes begin. Zoji La at 3,528 metres is where Kashmir ends and Ladakh begins, and the change in landscape on the far side is immediate and dramatic.

Drass is the first town of consequence on the Ladakh side. It holds the record as the second coldest inhabited place on earth, which gives it a certain distinction. The Kargil War Memorial at Vijaypath is one of the most moving war memorials in India, with photographs, personal effects, and an account of the 1999 conflict that is honest and detailed. Kargil town by evening. Overnight in Kargil.

Sonamarg (2,740m)Zoji La Pass (3,528m)Drass — Second Coldest Inhabited PlaceKargil War Memorial (Vijaypath)
Day 4
Kargil → Lamayuru → Leh

The drive from Kargil to Leh follows the Indus River for much of the way. Lamayuru Monastery is the most important stop: it is the oldest in Ladakh, founded in the 10th century, and it sits in the middle of an eroded moonscape of white clay that looks like nothing else on the planet. Local people call it Moonland and the name is apt. The monastery itself is active and the prayer hall contains murals that date back several centuries.

Sangam, where the Indus and Zanskar rivers meet, is an hour past Lamayuru. The Magnetic Hill optical illusion is just before Leh. Gurudwara Pathar Sahib and the Hall of Fame museum round out the day before arriving in Leh for the first overnight. The altitude here is 3,500 metres. Drink water and rest.

Day 5
Leh → Khardungla Pass → Nubra Valley → Hunder

Early morning departure for Khardungla at 18,380 feet. The summit is cold at any time of year. The descent into Nubra Valley is one of the great drives in India, taking you from the barren high-altitude pass down through increasingly green terrain to the valley floor where the Shyok River runs alongside irrigated fields.

Diskit Monastery, founded in the 14th century and the oldest in Nubra, sits on a rocky spur above the valley. The 32-metre Maitreya Buddha below it was built in 2010 and is visible from several kilometres. Hunder village, where sand dunes have accumulated in the cold desert between the mountains, is where the night is spent. Bactrian double-humped camels on the dunes at sunset are one of the more surreal images Ladakh offers.

Khardungla Pass (18,380 ft)Diskit MonasteryMaitreya Buddha StatueHunder Sand DunesCamel Safari (optional)
Day 6
Hunder → Turtuk → Thang Zero Point → Hunder

Turtuk is two hours from Hunder along the Shyok River. It opened to tourists in 2010 after decades of being off limits due to proximity to the Line of Control. The village is Balti, ethnically and culturally distinct from the rest of Ladakh. The lanes are tight, stone-paved, lined with apricot and mulberry trees. The people speak Balti, a language closer to Tibetan than to any Indian language. The houses are built of stone and mud with carved wooden balconies. Several families open their homes to visitors. The apricots from Turtuk are famous throughout Ladakh.

Beyond Turtuk, the road continues to Thang, also called Zero Point. This is as far as you can drive in India on this road. Pakistan is across the river. Return to Hunder for a second overnight in Nubra.

Turtuk Village (last Indian village, opened 2010)Balti Culture, Food and ArchitectureThang Zero Point — India-Pakistan Border
Day 7
Hunder → Pangong Lake via Shyok

The five-hour drive along the Shyok River to Pangong Tso is beautiful in a stripped-back, uncompromising way. The mountains are enormous and the valley is wide and the road is sometimes unpaved and there is almost no traffic. The lake arrives suddenly, vast and blue beyond the final rise in the road.

Pangong Tso at 4,350 metres is 134 kilometres long. Roughly two thirds of it is in China. The colour of the water has made it one of the most photographed lakes in Asia, and the photographs still do not prepare you for the actual scale of it. The camp at the lakeside is simple and cold at night. Both are part of the experience. Overnight at Pangong.

Shyok River Valley DriveTangste VillagePangong Tso (4,350m)India-China Border LakeLakeside Camp Overnight
Day 8
Pangong → Leh via Chang La Pass

One last morning at Pangong before the drive back to Leh via Chang La at 17,585 feet. The monastery country between Chang La and Leh is excellent. Thiksey, fifteen minutes from Leh, is built on a hill in the style of a Tibetan monastery town, each level housing monks' quarters, prayer halls, and storerooms. The golden face of the Maitreya Buddha inside is visible from the rooftop. Shey Palace is nearby, and the 3 Idiots filming location is the same road. Sindhu Darshan Ghat at the Indus River is a calm final stop. Last overnight in Leh.

Chang La Pass (17,585 ft)Thiksey MonasteryShey Palace and Monastery3 Idiots Filming LocationSindhu Darshan Ghat
Day 9
Departure from Leh

Early morning transfer to Leh Airport. Nine days across Kashmir and Ladakh, from Dal Lake to Pangong, from green valleys to cold deserts. There is always a reason to come back.
